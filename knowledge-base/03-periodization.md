## Periodization Principles

Periodization is the planned manipulation of training variables (volume, intensity, frequency) over time to maximize adaptation and prevent overtraining.

**The fundamental principle:** the body adapts to a specific stress. Once adapted, that stress must increase or change to drive further adaptation. Randomly varying workouts without progressive structure produces general fitness but not systematic improvement.

---

## Concurrent Periodization

CrossFit uses **concurrent periodization** — strength and conditioning are trained in the same sessions and the same week, not in separate blocks.

**Challenge:** Concurrent training creates an **interference effect** — endurance training can blunt strength and hypertrophy adaptations when both are trained heavily.

**How to minimize interference:**
- Separate strength and endurance work within the session (strength first)
- Avoid high-volume endurance work on the same day as heavy lower body lifting
- Keep metcon time domains short to medium
- Ensure adequate recovery between sessions

**Reference:** Wilson et al. "Concurrent Training: A Meta-Analysis Examining Interference of Aerobic and Resistance Exercises." *Journal of Strength and Conditioning Research* 26(8), 2012.

## Loading Parameters

### Intensity Zones (% of 1 Rep Max)

| Zone | % 1RM | Rep Range | Primary Adaptation |
|---|---|---|---|
| Strength-endurance | 50–65% | 15–20+ | Muscular endurance |
| Hypertrophy | 65–80% | 6–15 | Muscle size |
| Strength | 80–90% | 3–6 | Neural + structural strength |
| Maximal strength | 90–100% | 1–3 | Neural efficiency |
| Power | 30–70% (of max) | 1–5 (explosive) | Rate of force development |

### Progressive Loading by Week

For a 2–4 week program block, loading follows a linear progression within each cycle:

| Week | Intensity | Volume | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Week 1 | 70–75% 1RM | Moderate (4×5 or 5×3) | Technique + baseline load |
| Week 2 | 75–80% 1RM | Moderate-high (4×4 or 4×3) | Build |
| Week 3 | 80–85% 1RM | Lower reps (4×3 or 5×2) | Peak intensity |
| Week 4 (if applicable) | 60–65% 1RM | Low (3×5) | Deload — mandatory recovery |

**Rule:** A 4-week program must include a deload week. A 2-week program does not require one but should end at a lower relative intensity than it peaks.

### Rest Periods by Goal

| Goal | Rest Between Sets |
|---|---|
| Maximal strength | 3–5 minutes |
| Hypertrophy | 60–90 seconds |
| Power | 2–3 minutes (full PCr recovery) |
| Strength-endurance | 30–60 seconds |

## EMOM Strength Format

**EMOM (Every Minute On the Minute)** or **E2MOM/E3MOM (Every 2/3 Minutes)** can be used for strength blocks as an alternative to traditional timed rest periods.

### How It Works

Instead of prescribing "Rest 180s between sets," the athlete performs their set at the start of each interval and rests for the remainder.

**Example:**
```
E2MOM × 8 rounds (16 min total)
  2 Back Squats @ 80% 1RM
```

The athlete completes 2 squats, then rests until the next 2-minute mark. If the squats take 20 seconds, they rest 100 seconds. If they take 30 seconds, they rest 90 seconds.

### Benefits of EMOM Strength

1. **Auto-regulated rest** — faster execution earns more recovery time, rewarding efficiency
2. **Pacing discipline** — prevents sandbagging or excessively long rest periods
3. **Session flow** — keeps the strength block moving, no dead time
4. **Timer integration** — the PWA timer can manage this automatically
5. **Mental engagement** — the clock creates urgency and structure

### Programming Guidelines for EMOM Strength

| Interval | Appropriate Loading | Reps per Set | Total Rounds |
|---|---|---|---|
| E1MOM | 60-70% 1RM | 1-3 | 10-12 |
| E2MOM | 70-85% 1RM | 2-5 | 6-10 |
| E3MOM | 85-90% 1RM | 1-3 | 5-8 |

**Rest adequacy check:** The interval must provide sufficient phosphocreatine recovery. For heavy loading (>80% 1RM), use minimum E2MOM. For maximal loading (>90% 1RM), use E3MOM.

### When to Use EMOM vs Traditional Rest

- **EMOM strength:** Great for Week 1-2 baseline building, pacing practice, and moderate-heavy loads
- **Traditional rest:** Better for Week 3 peak intensity and maximal effort attempts where psychological readiness varies

**Rule for app:** Mix both formats across a program. Use EMOM for ~30-50% of strength sessions to add variety and keep athletes engaged with the timer.

**Reference:** This format is commonly used in CrossFit strength programming and supported by the same periodization principles as traditional rest intervals (Bompa & Haff - Periodization 5th ed).

## Intra-Session Progression Schemes

Instead of using the same weight across all sets (straight sets), intra-session progression varies loading within a single workout to target different adaptations or manage fatigue.

### Scheme 1: Ramp/Build-Up Sets

**Structure:** Progressively increase load across sets while decreasing reps
**Example:**
```
Back Squat (Ramp to Heavy)
Set 1: 5 reps @ 65% 1RM
Set 2: 4 reps @ 72% 1RM
Set 3: 3 reps @ 78% 1RM
Set 4: 2 reps @ 83% 1RM
```

**Purpose:**
- Prepare the nervous system for heavier loads
- Develop technique under progressively challenging weights
- Reduce injury risk by gradually approaching working sets
- Useful for complex movements (Olympic lifts, heavy squats)

**When to use:** Week 1 of programs, Olympic lifting sessions, athletes returning from layoff

**Reference:** Zatsiorsky & Kraemer describe progressive warm-up sets as essential for neural preparation and technique refinement under load (*Science and Practice of Strength Training*, Ch. 3).

---

### Scheme 2: Wave Loading

**Structure:** Oscillate between heavier and lighter loads across sets
**Example:**
```
Deadlift (Wave Loading)
Set 1: 3 reps @ 75% 1RM
Set 2: 3 reps @ 80% 1RM
Set 3: 3 reps @ 85% 1RM
Set 4: 3 reps @ 75% 1RM
Set 5: 3 reps @ 82% 1RM
Set 6: 3 reps @ 87% 1RM
```

**Purpose:**
- Exploit post-activation potentiation (PAP) — heavy sets prime the nervous system for subsequent performance
- Manage fatigue by alternating heavy and moderate loads
- Increase total volume at high intensities without linear fatigue accumulation
- Neural adaptation emphasis

**When to use:** Week 2-3 of programs, experienced athletes, strength emphasis phases

**Reference:** Robbins (2005) documents PAP effects lasting 3-10 minutes after heavy loading. Wave loading exploits this by following heavy sets with moderate loads that feel lighter due to neural priming (*Post-Activation Potentiation*).

---

### Scheme 3: Top Set + Back-Off Volume

**Structure:** One heavy "top set" followed by multiple lighter volume sets
**Example:**
```
Front Squat (Top Set + Volume)
Set 1: 2 reps @ 87% 1RM (top set)
Sets 2-5: 5 reps @ 70% 1RM (back-off volume)
```

**Purpose:**
- Combines neural stimulation (heavy top set) with hypertrophy/technique volume (back-off sets)
- Allows high-intensity exposure without excessive CNS fatigue
- Efficient for concurrent training — strength stimulus + volume accumulation
- Psychological benefit: heaviest work happens fresh

**When to use:** Week 3 peak intensity, intermediate athletes, limited time sessions

**Reference:** This approach is supported by Bompa & Haff's concurrent periodization model — neural work (heavy top set) followed by structural work (volume sets) within the same session minimizes interference while maximizing adaptation diversity (*Periodization 5th ed*, Ch. 9).

---

### Scheme 4: Straight Sets (Baseline)

**Structure:** Same weight and reps across all sets
**Example:**
```
Bench Press (Straight Sets)
5 sets × 5 reps @ 75% 1RM
```

**Purpose:**
- Simplest scheme, easy to track and execute
- Builds work capacity at a specific intensity
- Best for hypertrophy phases and baseline strength development
- Lower cognitive load for fatigued athletes

**When to use:** Week 1-2 baseline phases, hypertrophy emphasis, simple movements, novice athletes

**Reference:** Classic linear periodization structure from Bompa & Haff — moderate intensity with moderate volume for structural adaptation (*Periodization 5th ed*).

### Programming Guidelines for Progression Schemes

| Scheme | Best For | Week in Cycle | Movement Type | Athlete Level |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Ramp/Build-Up** | Neural prep, technique | Week 1, Olympic lifts | Complex movements | All levels |
| **Wave Loading** | Strength, PAP exploitation | Week 2-3 | Heavy compounds | Intermediate+ |
| **Top Set + Back-Off** | Time efficiency, peak + volume | Week 3, limited time | All barbell lifts | Intermediate+ |
| **Straight Sets** | Hypertrophy, baseline strength | Week 1-2 | Simple movements | All levels |

**Rule for app:** Vary progression schemes across the program to develop different qualities and maintain athlete engagement. Don't use the same scheme for all sessions. Match scheme to training goal, movement complexity, and week intensity.

Rate of Perceived Exertion (RPE) on a 1–10 scale:

| RPE | Description | Reps in Reserve |
|---|---|---|
| 10 | Maximum effort, could not do more | 0 |
| 9 | Could do 1 more rep | 1 |
| 8 | Could do 2 more reps | 2 |
| 7 | Could do 3 more reps | 3 |
| 6 | Moderate effort | 4+ |

**App rule:** When 1RM is not provided, prescribe RPE 7–8 for strength work in weeks 1–2, RPE 8–9 in week 3. Never prescribe RPE 10 in a program (reserve for testing only).

## Volume Guidelines

**Weekly volume per movement pattern (sets across all sessions):**

| Pattern | Minimum | Maximum | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Squat | 6 sets | 16 sets | Includes front squat, overhead squat |
| Hinge | 4 sets | 12 sets | Deadlift, Romanian DL, kettlebell swing |
| Push (horizontal) | 4 sets | 12 sets | Push-up, bench, dumbbell press |
| Push (vertical) | 4 sets | 10 sets | Press, HSPU, dumbbell shoulder press |
| Pull (horizontal) | 4 sets | 12 sets | Row variations |
| Pull (vertical) | 4 sets | 12 sets | Pull-up, ring row |
| Olympic | 4 sets | 10 sets | Clean, snatch, jerk variations |

**Reference:** Schoenfeld, B. *The Science and Development of Muscle Hypertrophy*, 2nd ed. Human Kinetics, 2020.

## Post-Activation Potentiation (PAP)

Heavy loaded movements (>85% 1RM) temporarily enhance neuromuscular output for 3–10 minutes afterward. This can be used to pair heavy strength work with explosive movements:

- Heavy back squat → box jumps
- Heavy deadlift → broad jumps
- Heavy press → push press or handstand push-up

**Mechanism:** Heavy loading increases motor unit recruitment and myosin phosphorylation, temporarily increasing force production capacity.

**Reference:** Robbins, D.W. "Postactivation Potentiation and Its Practical Applicability." *Journal of Strength and Conditioning Research* 19(2), 2005.

**Rule for app:** PAP pairings are used intentionally in strength blocks, not randomly. Always separated by 3+ minutes rest.
